Cyber warfare: China attacks force Taiwan to bolster defenses

TAIPEI -- In the wake of heightened Chinese aggression, Taiwan is beefing up its military capabilities, including raising defense spending by 15% next year. But pressure is also growing on Taiwan to build resilience to another kind of warfare that could wreak devastating damage -- cyberattacks.

The government and companies were targets of such attacks in connection with the controversial visit of U.S. House Speaker Nancy Pelosi at the beginning of August. Customers in 7-Eleven stores saw bulletin messages reading "Warmonger Pelosi, get out of Taiwan." And electronic billboards were hijacked across Taiwan -- one calling her an "old witch" whose visit is a "serious provocation to the sovereignty of the motherland." The websites of the presidential office and foreign affairs and defense ministries were also shut by hackers for a short while. While no real damage was done, the online offensive caused worry in Taiwan about whether its key infrastructure and essential services have strong enough firewalls and the ability to withstand determined cyberattacks. It came as China's biggest-ever military drills encircled the democratic island that Beijing regards as a renegade province but has never been controlled by Communist China.

"If power plants, hospitals, and transportation are hacked, the damage would be significant," Wang Ming-hung, an assistant professor of computer studies at National Chung Cheng University, told Nikkei Asia.

He said Taiwan's government, military and public should work to ensure they are prepared. "Everyone is exposed to the risks of cyberattacks," he said, "from sensitive data leakage to online service suspension and disinformation or misinformation to critical infrastructure."

(Screenshots from Facebook) Kuo Szu-Wei, a cybersecurity analyst at the Taipei-based Institute for Information Industry (III), shares Wang's concern. "All of the key infrastructure," Kuo told Nikkei, "hospitals, water and electricity stations as well as leading companies could be targets. "In the age of digital transformation, linking devices and systems to the internet is almost inevitable. That leads to regular software and firmware updates, which create vulnerabilities for attacks." Tseng Yisuo, a research fellow at Taiwan's Institute of National Defense and Security Research (INDSR), said the wave of cognitive warfare during China's recent live military drills was not unique. He called it an initial demonstration of China's "cyber warfare plan." While Taiwan takes the threat seriously and has implemented measures to deal with it, "Taiwan's ability to survive a major [cyber] attack has yet to be tested," said J. Michael Cole, a Taipei-based senior adviser on countering foreign authoritarian influence at the International Republican Institute. Taiwan's government has been stepping up efforts to combat cyberattacks. President Tsai Ing-wen has repeatedly declared "cybersecurity is national security." The government is forming a cybersecurity agency inside the newly established Ministry of Digital Affairs, which is headed by hacker-turned-cabinet member Audrey Tang. The agency will hire an initial 150 cybersecurity specialists, a sharp increase from the 20 staff it had at a cabinet-affiliated cybersecurity center. Hacking and fake news activities are widely expected to increase in frequency and in reach amid rising pressure from China, particularly as Taiwan heads toward local elections in November and presidential voting in January 2024. Cyber threats range from shutting down sites or taking control of systems to stealing user data.

"When hackers access and change the content of display screens in train stations and convenience shops and websites of universities, these attacks are serious," III's Kuo said. The incidents indicate that malware has already been planted and data could already have been stolen, he added.

The nongovernmental Information Operations Research Group (IORG) says its research on the information environment from Aug. 1 through Aug. 3 -- when Pelosi was in Taiwan -- shows an unusual volume of posts by Beijing. On official Chinese government websites as well as the social media accounts of state agencies, foreign ministry officers, and state and quasi-state media outlets, the visit was the topic of 17% of all posts. "This is much higher than past events," said Chihhao Yu, co-director of the Taipei-based IORG.
